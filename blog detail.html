More than 80 clinical trials launch to test of the coronavirus .

Naimur Rahman     05 Nov 2025
  05 Comments      33K Views

In early 2020, more than 80 clinical trials were launched in China to test a wide range of treatments for COVID-19, including repurposed antiviral drugs like remdesivir, lopinavir–ritonavir, and oseltamivir, as well as traditional Chinese medicines and novel therapies such as stem cells and immunoglobulins. These efforts aimed to quickly identify safe and effective treatments for the rapidly spreading virus. Some early lab studies and patient cases, especially involving remdesivir, showed promising results, prompting global interest.

However, not all drugs proved effective—for instance, oseltamivir was widely used but later excluded from treatment guidelines due to a lack of benefit. To coordinate international research, the World Health Organization launched the Solidarity trial to test the most promising therapies on a global scale. The goal of these trials was to provide evidence-based solutions to manage and reduce the severity of COVID-19 cases.

In response to the outbreak of COVID-19 in early 2020, China launched over 80 clinical trials aimed at finding effective treatments for the virus. These trials tested a variety of approaches, including repurposed antiviral drugs such as lopinavir–ritonavir, oseltamivir, ganciclovir, and the experimental drug remdesivir. Many of these medications had previously been used to treat other viral infections like HIV, influenza, or Ebola, and were quickly brought into trials in hopes of fighting the novel coronavirus.

Alongside modern antivirals, traditional Chinese medicines and herbal treatments were also included in several trials, reflecting China's integrated approach to healthcare. Some combinations, like arbidol with lopinavir–ritonavir, were designed to explore possible synergy between drugs.

Early findings brought cautious optimism. For example, remdesivir showed encouraging results in lab tests and in the case of a U.S. patient who improved after receiving it intravenously. However, not all treatments proved useful. Oseltamivir, commonly used for influenza, was initially included in many treatment protocols but was later dropped due to a lack of clinical benefit.

To streamline and coordinate the global search for effective treatments, the World Health Organization initiated the Solidarity trial. This large, multinational study focused on the most promising therapies, including remdesivir, lopinavir–ritonavir (with or without interferon), and chloroquine or hydroxychloroquine. The goal of these trials was not only to treat individual patients but also to generate high-quality evidence that could guide treatment guidelines around the world.
